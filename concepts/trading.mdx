Trading on Puls means taking a position on whether a real-world event will happen. You buy shares that represent your view — YES if you think the event will occur, NO if you think it won't — and you profit when the market resolves in your favour. Because share prices move with market sentiment, you can also trade actively before resolution, locking in gains or cutting losses as the probability shifts.

## YES and NO Shares

Every Puls market offers exactly two types of shares: **YES** and **NO**. Their prices are complementary — they always add up to $1.00 — and each price directly represents the market's implied probability for that outcome.

- A **YES share priced at 60¢** means the market estimates a 60% chance the event happens.
- The corresponding **NO share is priced at 40¢**, reflecting a 40% chance it doesn't.

Share prices float between $0.01 and $0.99 as traders buy and sell. When you buy YES shares, you push the YES price up (and NO price down), signalling increased confidence in the outcome. When you buy NO shares, the reverse occurs.

## How Returns Work

Each share pays out exactly **$1.00** if it resolves in your favour, or **$0.00** if it resolves against you. Your return depends on the price you paid and which way the market settles.

| Scenario | Cost per Share | Payout per Share | Return |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buy YES @ 60¢, resolves YES | $0.60 | $1.00 | +$0.40 (+67%) |
| Buy YES @ 60¢, resolves NO | $0.60 | $0.00 | −$0.60 (−100%) |
| Buy NO @ 40¢, resolves NO | $0.40 | $1.00 | +$0.60 (+150%) |
| Buy NO @ 40¢, resolves YES | $0.40 | $0.00 | −$0.40 (−100%) |

The less likely the market thinks an outcome is, the lower the share price — and the higher your potential return if you're right. This is what makes contrarian positions both riskier and more rewarding than consensus ones.

## Position Sizing

How much you trade in any single market should reflect your confidence level and your overall risk tolerance. A few principles to guide you:

- **Start small on unfamiliar topics.** Markets you know well are where an edge is most likely to exist. On topics where you have no informational advantage, smaller positions limit your downside.
- **Diversify across markets.** Concentrating too much capital in a single market amplifies the impact of a single bad outcome. Spreading trades across multiple markets smooths your overall results.
- **Consider liquidity.** In lower-volume markets, large orders can move the price significantly against you before your trade is complete. Check the market depth before sizing up. For more on this, see [Liquidity and Market Depth](/concepts/liquidity).

There is no single right position size — the goal is to size trades so that no individual loss materially harms your overall portfolio.

## Selling Before Resolution

You don't have to hold your shares until a market resolves. As long as a market is **Open**, you can sell your position at the current market price and realise your gain or loss immediately.

Selling early is useful when:

- **The probability has moved in your favour** and you want to lock in a profit without waiting for the event to occur.
- **New information changes your view** and you'd rather exit than risk holding through an unfavourable resolution.
- **You need your capital back** for a higher-conviction opportunity elsewhere.

When you sell, your shares are bought by the market at the prevailing price. You receive the sale proceeds instantly, and your position is closed. Note that once a market moves to **Closed** or **Resolving** status, early exit is no longer available.

<Warning>
  Prediction markets carry real financial risk. You can lose the full amount you invest in a position if the market resolves against you. Never trade more than you can afford to lose, and ensure you understand the resolution criteria of a market before placing a trade.
</Warning>
