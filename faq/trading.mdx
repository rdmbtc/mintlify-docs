Trading on Puls is straightforward once you understand a few core concepts. Whether you're puzzling over why a price moved or wondering how to close out a position early, the answers below cover the mechanics of buying, selling, and everything in between.

<Accordion title="How do market prices work?">
  Every market on Puls has at least two outcomes — for example, YES and NO — and each outcome has a share price that fluctuates between $0.01 and $0.99. The price reflects the market's current implied probability that the outcome will occur: a YES price of $0.70 means traders collectively believe there's roughly a 70% chance the event happens. Prices update in real time as new trades are placed.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What does 'Buy YES' and 'Buy NO' mean?">
  Buying YES means you believe the event described in the market will happen — you're purchasing shares that pay out $1.00 each if the market resolves YES. Buying NO means you believe the event will *not* happen, and those shares pay out $1.00 each if the market resolves NO. You profit when the outcome you backed turns out to be correct.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Can I sell my shares before a market resolves?">
  Yes — one of the key features of Puls is the ability to exit a position at any time while the market is still open. Simply navigate to your portfolio, select the market, and choose **Sell**. Your shares are sold at the current market price, which may be higher or lower than what you originally paid, locking in a profit or limiting a loss before the final outcome is known.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Are there fees for trading?">
  Puls may charge a small fee on each trade — check our Fees page for the current fee schedule before you trade. Any applicable fee is displayed on the trade confirmation screen before you commit, so you'll always know your cost upfront. We recommend reviewing the Fees page periodically, as rates may be updated over time.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Is there a minimum or maximum trade size?">
  The minimum trade size on Puls is $1.00, making it easy to get started with a small stake while you learn the ropes. Maximum trade sizes vary by market and are set to ensure healthy liquidity for all participants. You'll see the applicable limits shown directly on each market's trading panel.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What happens if a market closes before I place my trade?">
  Once a market's closing time passes, no new trades can be entered. If you attempt to place a trade on a closed market, it will be declined and your funds will remain in your account untouched. We recommend setting price alerts or monitoring markets you're interested in so you don't miss your window.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Why did the price change after I bought?">
  Prices on Puls move continuously based on new orders from other traders. The moment you complete a purchase, your own order shifts the supply and demand balance, and subsequent trades by other users can push the price further. This is normal market behavior — prices reflect the collective, real-time sentiment of everyone trading in that market.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What is slippage?">
  Slippage is the difference between the price you expected to receive and the price at which your trade actually executes. It typically occurs in markets with lower liquidity, where a large order can move the price as it fills — meaning the last shares in your order cost more (or pay less) than the first. You can reduce slippage by splitting large trades into smaller ones or by using the maximum slippage tolerance setting in the advanced trade panel.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Can I place multiple trades on the same market?">
  Absolutely — you can buy additional shares in a market as many times as you like while it remains open, and your holdings will be combined in your portfolio. This lets you average into a position as new information emerges or add to a trade you're feeling confident about. Keep in mind that each individual trade may incur its own fee per the current fee schedule.
</Accordion>
